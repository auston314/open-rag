Q: What is the purpose of the U.S. Leave of Absence Policy?
A: The purpose of the U.S. Leave of Absence Policy for Activision Blizzard, Inc. is to establish guidelines governing leaves of absence for eligible U.S. employees and to ensure that such leaves are granted and administered in accordance with applicable federal, state, and local laws. This policy covers non-vacation time off that exceeds five business days, whether consecutive or intermittent, for a single purpose. For additional time off benefits, employees are directed to review the SupportDesk Article: “US Time Away From Work”.

Q: What types of time off are covered under this policy?
A: The policy covers several types of time off related to leaves of absence that exceed five business days. The types of time off include:

1. **Family Medical Leave**: Unpaid, job-protected leave under the FMLA, up to 12 or 26 workweeks.
2. **Domestic Partner Leave**: Unpaid leave for caring for a domestic partner with a serious health condition, up to 12 workweeks.

7. **Workers’ Compensation Leave**: Leave for workplace injuries or illnesses.
8. **Leave as an Accommodation**: Unpaid leave for a qualifying disability or medical condition under the ADA.
9. **Personal Leave of Absence**: Discretionary unpaid leave for non-medical reasons, up to 26 weeks in a 3-year period.
10. **Other Leaves Required by Law**: Additional leaves as required, such as extended bereavement leave.

Q: How are leaves of absence granted under the policy?
A: Leaves of absence are granted under the policy on a non-discriminatory basis to all eligible U.S. employees and are administered in accordance with applicable federal, state, and local laws. Key provisions include:

1. **Concurrent Running of Leaves**: Whenever statutory or other leave provisions overlap, they will run concurrently to the fullest extent permitted by law.
2. **Employment Status**: Granting a leave of absence does not change the at-will nature of an employee’s employment, and the Company reserves the right to amend the policy at any time, consistent with the law.
3. **Prohibited Activities**: Employees may not accept additional employment, apply for unemployment insurance, or engage in activities inconsistent with the stated reason for the leave unless otherwise approved by Human Resources. Misrepresentation regarding the need for leave may lead to disciplinary action.
4. **Return to Work Obligation**: Employees are expected to return to work promptly upon the expiration of their approved leave. Failure to return or to contact the Company within three consecutive business days after the expiration of leave may be considered job abandonment.
5. **Non-discrimination and Non-retaliation**: The Company will not interfere with, deny, or discriminate against employees exercising their rights under this policy. Employees are protected from retaliation for using or requesting leave, filing complaints, or participating in an investigation related to their leave rights.

**General Procedure for Requesting Leaves**:
1. Notify Aflac and the Company’s Leaves Team with the request for most leave types.
2. Provide supporting information and documentation, including the anticipated dates and duration of the leave.
3. Submit leave requests with appropriate timing, giving at least 30 days' notice if foreseeable.
4. Leave requests must be approved by Aflac after reviewing all necessary information.

**Specific Circumstances**:
- For retroactive leave requests, submit no more than seven days after the leave begins.
- The "rolling" 12-month period method is used, measured backward from the start date of leave unless otherwise stated.

For detailed information, employees are encouraged to contact the Leaves Team or review the respective sections of the leave policy.

Q: How are paid and unpaid leaves treated under the policy?
A: The policy outlines the treatment of paid and unpaid leaves in various sections, but here is a summary:

### General Treatment
1. **Most leaves of absence are unpaid**, except as specifically provided by the policy or required by law. Detailed information about the paid or unpaid status of each leave type is provided in the relevant sections of the policy.
   
2. **Use of Paid Leave**: The Company may require employees to use all applicable Health and Vacation time (collectively referred to as "paid time off") towards an otherwise unpaid or partially paid leave, not to exceed 100% of the employee’s regular pay. If the Company does not require this, an employee may still opt to substitute paid time off for unpaid or partially paid leave, provided they comply with the Company’s procedures.

3. **Integration with Disability Plans**: Employees may be able to integrate short- or long-term disability leave plans with otherwise unpaid portions of medical leave. Contact the Leaves Team for details on the availability of benefits, including state-paid disability and paid family and medical leave programs.
   
4. **Effect on Accrual**: Employees do not accrue vacation benefits during a leave of absence, unless required by law. However, those who use one full week of paid leave per week during a leave will continue to accrue vacation time at their regular rates.

### Maintenance of Health Benefits
During a qualified leave, the Company will maintain group health plan coverage on the same terms as if the employee were working, up to a maximum of 12 months. Employees must continue to pay their share of health plan premiums. If paid leave is used, benefit contributions are deducted from pay. If paid leave runs out, the employee must pay contributions directly (such as through payroll deductions upon return).

### Specific Leaves
1. **Family Medical Leave Act (FMLA)**: Generally unpaid, but employees may be eligible for state-sponsored benefits or use available paid time off. Any wage-replacement benefits and paid time off are integrated so that employees do not receive more than their regular compensation. The use of paid benefits does not extend FMLA Leave duration.

2. **Domestic Partner Leave**: Unpaid but may be supplemented by state-sponsored benefits or available paid time off. Payments and benefits are integrated so that employees receive no more than their regular compensation.

3. **Company Paid Parental Leave**: Company-paid benefit of up to 10 workweeks. Employees are not required to use their paid time off during this period.

4. **Company Paid Compassion Leave**: Company-paid benefit of up to 10 workweeks.

5. **Military Leave for Employee’s Own Military Service**: Employees regularly scheduled for at least thirty (30) hours a week are eligible for military differential pay for up to twelve (12) months. Unpaid leave follows the exhaustion of differential pay.

6. **Military-Related FMLA Leave**: Generally unpaid, but may be eligible for integrated wage-replacement benefits and use of paid time off, up to regular compensation.

7. **Workers’ Compensation Leave**: Employees do not receive pay from the Company; instead, workers’ compensation benefits are paid through the insurance carrier.

8. **Leave of Absence as an Accommodation (ADA Leave)**: Unpaid but employees may use and exhaust available paid time off as permitted by law.

9. **Personal Leave of Absence**: Unpaid leave, but employees are required to use and exhaust their paid time off during this period.

For more specific details and to understand how each type of leave affects pay, accruals, and benefits during leave, employees should refer to the relevant sections within the policy.

Q: What is covered under the Family Medical Leave Act (FMLA)?
A: The Family Medical Leave Act (FMLA) at Activision Blizzard entitles eligible employees to take unpaid, job-protected leave for specified family and medical reasons, while ensuring the continuation of group health insurance coverage under the same terms and conditions as if the employee had not taken leave. Here are the key points covered under the FMLA:

### A. Eligibility:
To be eligible for FMLA Leave, an employee must:
- Have worked for the Company for at least 12 months prior to the leave start date.
- Have worked at least 1,250 hours in the past 12 months prior to the leave start date.

### B. Reasons for Taking FMLA Leave:
1. Up to twelve (12) workweeks of leave in a 12-month period may be taken for:
   - The birth of a child and to care for the newborn within one year of birth.
   - The placement with the employee of a child for adoption or foster care and to care for the newly placed child within one year of placement.
   - To care for the employee’s spouse, child, or parent who has a serious health condition.
   - A serious health condition that makes the employee unable to perform the essential functions of their job.
   - Any qualifying exigency arising out of the fact that the employee’s spouse, child, or parent is a covered military member on “covered active duty”. (See Section 9, “Military-Related FMLA Leave”).

2. Up to twenty-six (26) workweeks of leave during a single 12-month period may be taken to care for a covered servicemember with a serious injury or illness if the eligible employee is the servicemember’s spouse, child, or next of kin (military caregiver leave). (See Section 9, “Military-Related FMLA Leave”).

### C. Certification:
- All requests for FMLA Leave based on an employee’s serious health condition or the serious health condition of a covered family member must be supported by medical certification from a healthcare provider. Requests for FMLA leave for other purposes may also require certification.
- Certification forms will be provided by Aflac. Certification must generally be provided before the leave begins, or for unforeseeable leaves, within fifteen (15) calendar days of the Company’s or Aflac’s request (additional time may be permitted under certain circumstances).
- If an employee fails or refuses to provide the required medical certification within the required time, the Company may delay the commencement of the employee’s leave, withdraw any designation of FMLA Leave, or deny the leave, in which case the employee’s time away from work would be treated as unapproved, potentially subjecting the employee to disciplinary action, up to and including termination of employment.
- Aflac may request a second or third opinion from a health care provider.
- Requests for leave extensions beyond the period supported by the medical certification require an updated medical certification, which must be submitted to Aflac prior to the expiration of the leave.

### D. Intermittent FMLA Leave:
- FMLA Leave for the birth, adoption, or foster care placement of a child requires at least seven (7) days’ advance notice and must be completed within one (1) year of the birth, adoption, or placement.
- FMLA Leave for the employee’s or a family member's serious health condition may be taken intermittently or on a reduced schedule where medically necessary and must be reported to Aflac within two (2) days of each separate occurrence.
- Aflac may request certification or recertification of the need for intermittent leave under certain circumstances.

### E. FMLA Leave’s Effect on Pay:
- Generally, FMLA Leave is unpaid. Employees may be eligible for state-sponsored benefits or may use their available paid time off balance. All payments of wage-replacement benefits and paid time off will be integrated so employees receive no more than their regular compensation during their leave.
- Using paid benefits will not extend the length of an FMLA Leave unless otherwise required by law.

### G. Benefits During FMLA Leave:
- The Company will continue making contributions to employee group health benefits during FMLA Leave on the same terms as if the employee had continued working. Employees must also continue to make the same premium contributions they would make while actively working.
- The Company may recover premiums it paid on the employee’s behalf if the employee fails to return to work following FMLA Leave.

### H. Amount of FMLA Leave:
- Employees may take a maximum of twelve (12) workweeks of FMLA Leave (or twenty-six (26) workweeks for military caregiver leave) in a rolling 12-month period measured backward from the date the employee’s leave starts.
- Use of paid time off during FMLA Leave does not extend the total duration of job protection beyond the maximum FMLA Leave period.

### I. Returning to Work:
- Before returning to work from any medical leave for the employee’s own serious health condition, the employee must provide a medical provider’s release to return to work to Aflac or the Leaves Team.
- An employee returning from a qualified protected leave of absence in a timely manner is entitled to reinstatement in the same or comparable position.
- If the employee is unable to perform the essential functions of their position upon their return from Leave, the Company will engage in an interactive process to identify possible reasonable accommodations or attempt to locate an alternative position for which they are qualified, as required by law.

Reference: Family Medical Leave Act (FMLA)

   - A "serious injury or illness" for a current servicemember means one that may render them unfit to perform their military duties. For a veteran, it is an injury or illness that substantially impairs their ability to work or qualifies them for certain VA benefits.

Q: How is a Personal Leave of Absence different from other types of leave?
A: A Personal Leave of Absence is a discretionary and unprotected leave that is not related to medical reasons and is not protected under laws such as FMLA (Family Medical Leave Act) or other state or federal statutes. Here are the primary ways it differs from other types of leave described in the policy:

1. **Nature and Reason**:
   - **Personal Leave of Absence**: This leave is non-medical and granted at the company's discretion for compelling reasons when the leave does not create an unreasonable burden on the company’s business.
   - **Other Leaves (such as FMLA, Domestic Partner Leave, Parental Leave, Compassion Leave, etc.)**: These leaves are usually related to medical conditions, family care, parental duties, or specific legal entitlements and protections defined by federal or state laws.

2. **Duration**:
   - **Personal Leave of Absence**: The total leave time may not exceed 26 weeks in any 3-year period.
   - **Other Leaves**: The duration is often defined by law or company policy based on the type of leave (e.g., up to 12 weeks for FMLA, up to 10 weeks for Company Paid Parental Leave, etc.).

3. **Protection**:
   - **Personal Leave of Absence**: It is unprotected, meaning it is not legally required to be granted and does not offer job protection guarantees.
   - **Other Leaves**: Many other types of leave mentioned in the policy are protected by laws (like FMLA) or company guarantees, ensuring employees can return to their job or a comparable position.

4. **Pay**:
   - **Personal Leave of Absence**: This leave is unpaid, but employees are required to use and/or exhaust their paid time off (PTO) during the leave.
   - **Other Leaves**: The pay status varies, with some being paid (e.g., Company Paid Parental Leave), some unpaid (e.g., FMLA), or offering partial wage replacement through state programs.

Q: What is California Pregnancy Disability Leave (PDL)?
A: California Pregnancy Disability Leave (PDL) entitles eligible employees to up to four months (17 1/3 weeks) of disability leave due to pregnancy, childbirth, or a related medical condition.

### A. Eligibility
In California, any employee disabled due to pregnancy, childbirth or related conditions may take PDL for the period of time they are actually disabled, up to a maximum of four months (17 1/3 weeks) (693 hours for employees working a 5-day per week schedule) per pregnancy.

All requests for PDL, transfer, or accommodation must be certified by medical certification from a health care provider.

### E. Intermittent Leave
PDL may be taken intermittently or on a reduced schedule, as required by law.

Q: What is Washington Paid Family and Medical Leave (WA PFML)?
A: **Washington Paid Family and Medical Leave (WA PFML)** entitles eligible employees to take leave with partial wage replacement for qualifying reasons.

### Eligibility
To be eligible for WA PFML, the employee must:
- Experience a qualifying event; and
- Work 820 hours within the qualifying period.

“Qualifying period” means the first four out of the last five completed calendar quarters or, if eligibility is not established, the last four completed quarters immediately before the employee applies for leave.

### Reasons for Taking WA PFML
Qualifying reasons for taking WA PFML include:
- Bonding with the employee’s child during the first 12 months following the child's birth, or the first 12 months after the placement of a child under the age of 18 with the employee for adoption or foster care;

- Attending to a “qualifying exigency,” as defined under the federal Family and Medical Leave Act (“FMLA”), arising from the “covered active duty” (as defined by the WA PFML) of an employee’s covered family member as a member of the military reserves, National Guard, or Armed Forces;
- During the seven (7) calendar days following the death of a family member for whom the employee would have qualified for medical leave due to the birth of the employee’s child or would have qualified for family leave for the purpose of bonding following the child’s birth or placement.

“Covered Family Member” means the employee’s spouse or state-registered domestic partner, child, parent, grandparent, grandchild, or sibling. ### Runs Concurrently with Other Leave
When both the FMLA and the WA PFML apply, the leave provided by each will count against the employee’s entitlement under both laws, and leave taken under the FMLA will run concurrently with leave taken under the WA PFML.

WA PFML is in addition to any leave for sickness or temporary disability because of pregnancy or childbirth. When an employee takes leave for pregnancy disability under both the WA PFML and Pregnancy Disability Leave, the two leaves will run concurrently, but an employee’s WA PFML entitlement does not limit the amount of Pregnancy Disability Leave the employee may be entitled to.

### Certification
All requests for WA PFML based on the serious health condition of an employee or covered family member must be supported by medical certification from a health care provider. Supporting documentation may also be required for WA PFML taken for the purpose of new child bonding time or military leave.

### Intermittent Leave
- WA PFML may be taken intermittently for the birth or placement of a child. Bonding leave must be concluded within one (1) year of the qualifying event.
- Each absence for intermittent leave must be reported to Aflac within two (2) days of each separate occurrence.

### WA PFML’s Effect on Pay
WA PFML is determined and administered by the Employment Security Department (“Department”), not the Company. The Company will not require employees to use paid time off benefits before, in place of, or concurrently with WA PFML. An employee may choose whether to take Company-provided paid time off; however, such benefits will be supplemental to WA PFML and an employee may not use more supplemental benefits than will be required to provide the employee with a total of 100% of their pay for the absence. In any week in which an employee is eligible to receive benefits under federal or state unemployment compensation, industrial insurance, or disability insurance laws, the employee may be disqualified from receiving WA PFML wage replacement benefits.

### Amount of WA PFML
In a “Benefit Year”, employees may be entitled to one of the following:
- 12 weeks of paid family leave;
- 12 weeks of paid medical leave;
- 14 weeks of paid medical leave if the employee experiences a pregnancy-related serious health condition that results in incapacity;
- 16 weeks of combined paid family and paid medical leave; or
- 18 weeks of combined paid family and paid medical leave if the employee experiences a pregnancy-related serious health condition that results in incapacity.

“Benefit Year” means a period of 52 consecutive calendar weeks beginning on Sunday of the week of the employee’s timely and complete application to the Department. Employees will only have one “Benefit Year” at a time. For employees who are eligible for leave based upon incapacity due to pregnancy or for prenatal care, leave taken during the first six (6) weeks after birth (the “postnatal period”) will be presumed to be paid medical leave, unless the employee’s medical leave entitlement is fully or partially exhausted prior to the child’s birth, or the employee chooses to use paid family leave, if available, during that postnatal period.

Employees may obtain a brochure discussing their rights and obligations under the WA PFML program and a WA PFML benefits claim form from the Washington State Paid Family Leave website or from the Leaves Team.

Q: How is WA PFML’s effect on pay managed?
A: Washington Paid Family and Medical Leave (WA PFML) is determined and administered by the Employment Security Department (the "Department"), not Activision Blizzard. Here are the key points regarding WA PFML’s effect on pay:

1. **Choice of Taking Paid Time Off**: The company does not require employees to use their paid time off benefits before, in place of, or concurrently with WA PFML.
2. **Supplemental Benefits**: Employees may choose to take company-provided paid time off. However, these benefits will be supplemental to WA PFML, and an employee cannot use more supplemental benefits than needed to provide a total of 100% of their pay for the absence.
3. **Disqualification for Certain Benefits**: In any week when an employee is eligible to receive benefits under federal or state unemployment compensation, industrial insurance, or disability insurance laws, the employee may be disqualified from receiving WA PFML wage replacement benefits.
4. **Waiting Period**: For WA PFML taken for reasons other than family leave for a qualifying exigency, medical leave taken upon the birth of a child, or family leave taken for bonding after birth or placement of the child, there is a waiting period of seven consecutive calendar days before wage replacement benefits are paid. The waiting period begins on the Sunday of the first week an eligible employee starts taking paid family or medical leave. This waiting period does not reduce the maximum duration of paid family or medical leave available. Employees may use available vacation, sick leave (if applicable), or other company-provided paid time off during the waiting period.

Q: What additional supports are provided for employees in specific states?
A: The policy document provides additional supports for employees in specific states through state-specific appendices. Here is a summary for each state mentioned:

### California (Appendix A)
1. **California Family Rights Act (CFRA) Leave:** Provides up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave for specified family and medical reasons within a 12-month period.
2. **California Pregnancy Disability Leave (PDL):** Entitles employees up to four months (17 1/3 weeks) of disability leave due to pregnancy, childbirth, or a related medical condition.
3. **San Francisco Supplemental Compensation for New Child Bonding:** Offers supplemental compensation during employee’s leave for new child bonding if receiving California Paid Family Leave (PFL) benefits.
4. **San Francisco Military Leave Pay Protection:** Provides supplemental pay for up to 30 days of military duty leave in a calendar year.

### Massachusetts (Appendix B)
1. **Massachusetts Paid Family and Medical Leave (MA PFML):** Entitles eligible employees to up to 26 weeks of job-protected, partial-paid leave for specified family and medical reasons within a rolling 12-month period.

### New Jersey (Appendix C)
1. **New Jersey Family Leave Insurance (NJ FLI):** Offers up to 12 weeks of state-provided family leave insurance benefits for qualifying reasons such as bonding with a new child or caring for a family member with a serious health condition.

### New York (Appendix D)
1. **New York Paid Family Leave (NY PFL):** Allows eligible employees to take up to 12 workweeks of job-protected, paid leave for specific reasons such as bonding with a new child, caring for a family member, or addressing military exigencies.

### Washington State (Appendix E)
1. **Washington Paid Family and Medical Leave (WA PFML):** Provides partial wage replacement for qualifying family and medical leaves, with specific entitlements such as 12 to 18 weeks of combined leave under certain conditions.

Each appendix includes detailed eligibility criteria, reasons for taking leave, information on necessary certifications, and the effects of leave on pay and benefits.

Reference: Appendix A-E, State Appendices.
